## Given

An undirected graph $G = (V, E)$.

## Problem

Does $G$ have a perfect matching? A perfect matching is a matching where each vertex is incident to one edge in the matching.

## Status

Open (Karp, Upfal, and Wigderson <a href="#ref-1" class="reference-citation">[1]</a>, Mulmuley, Vazirani, and Vazirani <a href="#ref-2" class="reference-citation">[2]</a>).

## Remarks

See remarks for Problem B.9.7. PME seems to be the simplest of the matching problems not known to be in $\NC$. Dahlhaus, Hajnal, and Karpinski show that a perfect matching can be found in a "dense" graph in $\NC^2$ <a href="#ref-3" class="reference-citation">[3]</a>. Karpinski and Wagner show that when $G$ is given by its vertex multiplicity graph representation the problem becomes $\P$-complete <a href="#ref-4" class="reference-citation">[4]</a>. Using such succinct graph representations they are also able to show that the Perfect Bipartite Matching Problem is $\P$-complete <a href="#ref-4" class="reference-citation">[4]</a>. Grigoriev and Karpinski show that if the permanent of $G$ is polynomially bounded, one can decide whether $G$ has a perfect matching in $\NC^2$. They show how to construct such a matching in $\NC^3$ <a href="#ref-5" class="reference-citation">[5]</a>. See Grigoriev, Karpinski, and Singer <a href="#ref-6" class="reference-citation">[6]</a> and Karpinski <a href="#ref-7" class="reference-citation">[7]</a> for additional related work. Osiakwan and Akl designed an EREW-PRAM algorithm for solving the Maximum Weight Perfect Matching Problem for complete weighted graphs. Their algorithm runs in $O(n^3/p + n^2 \log n)$ time for $p \leq n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices in the graph and $p$ is the number of processors <a href="#ref-8" class="reference-citation">[8]</a>.
