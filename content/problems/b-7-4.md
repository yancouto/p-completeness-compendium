## Given

An $n$th degree polynomial $p \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$.

## Problem

Compute the factorization of $p$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.

## Status

Open problem from Greenlaw et al. (1995), Problem B.7.4.

## Remarks

UPFQ is $\text{NC}^1$ reducible to Short Vectors, Problem B.7.1 [1].

**B.8 CC**

Several researchers (including Anderson, Cook, Gupta, Mayr, and Subramanian) suggested looking at the complexity of the Comparator Circuit Value Problem, or $\text{CCVP}$, Problem B.8.1. This problem is defined below, Problem B.8.1. $\text{CC}$ is defined as the class of problems $\text{NC}$ many-one reducible to $\text{CCVP}$ (Mayr and Subramanian [2]). Problems in this section are all equivalent to, that is, $\text{NC}$ many-one interreducible with $\text{CCVP}$.

While the evidence is less compelling than that for $\text{RNC}$ problems (see Section B.9), it is generally considered unlikely that these problems are $\text{P}$-complete, because of the lack of fanout in comparator circuits. On the other hand, no fast parallel algorithms are known for them, with the partial exception of $\sqrt{n}(\log n)^{O(1)}$ algorithms for $\text{CCVP}$ and some related problems. Such algorithms were independently discovered by Danny Soroker (unpublished) and by Mayr and Subramanian [2]. Richard J. Anderson (also unpublished) has improved the algorithms so as to use only $\sqrt{n}$ processors. Mayr and Subramanian note that these algorithms are $\text{P}$-complete, in the sense of Chapter 8.

It is known that $\text{NLOG} \subseteq \text{CC} \subseteq \text{P}$ [2]. At present $\text{NC}$ and $\text{CC}$ are incomparable as are $\text{RNC}$ and $\text{CC}$. That is, the classes cannot be related using the $\subseteq$ relation. The majority of the results described in this appendix come from Mayr and Subramanian [2]. See also (Feder [3], Ramachandran and Wang [4], Subramanian [5, 6]) for more research on $\text{CC}$.
