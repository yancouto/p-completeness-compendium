## Given

An $n^\text{th}$ degree polynomial $p \in \Q[x]$.

## Problem

Compute the factorization of $p$ over $\Q$.

## Status

Open problem from Greenlaw et al. (1995), Problem B.7.4.

## Remarks

UPFQ is $\NC^1$ reducible to Short Vectors, Problem B.7.1 [1].

**B.8 CC**

Several researchers (including Anderson, Cook, Gupta, Mayr, and Subramanian) suggested looking at the complexity of the Comparator Circuit Value Problem, or [CCVP]({{< relref "./b-8-1.md" >}}), Problem B.8.1. This problem is defined below, Problem B.8.1. $\CC$ is defined as the class of problems $\NC$ many-one reducible to [CCVP]({{< relref "./b-8-1.md" >}}) (Mayr and Subramanian [2]). Problems in this section are all equivalent to, that is, $\NC$ many-one interreducible with [CCVP]({{< relref "./b-8-1.md" >}}).

While the evidence is less compelling than that for $\mathsf{RNC}$ problems (see Section B.9), it is generally considered unlikely that these problems are $\P$-complete, because of the lack of fanout in comparator circuits. On the other hand, no fast parallel algorithms are known for them, with the partial exception of $\sqrt{n}(\log n)^{O(1)}$ algorithms for [CCVP]({{< relref "./b-8-1.md" >}}) and some related problems. Such algorithms were independently discovered by Danny Soroker (unpublished) and by Mayr and Subramanian [2]. Richard J. Anderson (also unpublished) has improved the algorithms so as to use only $\sqrt{n}$ processors. Mayr and Subramanian note that these algorithms are $\P$-complete, in the sense of Chapter 8.

It is known that $\mathsf{NLOG} \subseteq \CC \subseteq \P$ [2]. At present $\NC$ and $\CC$ are incomparable as are $\mathsf{RNC}$ and $\CC$. That is, the classes cannot be related using the $\subseteq$ relation. The majority of the results described in this appendix come from Mayr and Subramanian [2]. See also (Feder [3], Ramachandran and Wang [4], Subramanian [5][6]) for more research on $\CC$.
