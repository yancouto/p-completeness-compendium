## Given

A fixed rank pebble game $G = (X, R, S, t)$.

## Problem

Does player $P_1$ have a winning strategy?

## Definitions

A pebble game $G = (X, R, S, t)$ has the following properties:

1. $X$ is a finite set of vertices.
2. $R \subseteq \\{(x, y, z) \mid x, y, z \in X, x \neq y, y \neq z, z \neq x\\}$ is called the set of *rules*. For $A, B \subseteq X$, we write $A \vdash B$ if $(x, y, z) \in R$, $x, y \in A$, $z \notin A$, and $B = (A \setminus \\{x\\}) \cup \\{z\\}$. The *move* $A \vdash B$ is made by the rule $(x, y, z)$. The symbol $\vdash^\ast$ denotes the reflexive and transitive closure of $\vdash$.
3. $S$ is a subset of $X$.
4. $t \in X$ is the *terminal* vertex.

A pebble game has *fixed rank* if the number of vertices in $S$ is fixed. In a *two-person* pebble game two players $P_1$ and $P_2$ alternate moving pebbles with $P_1$ moving first. The *winner* is the first player who can pebble $t$ or who can force the other player into a no move situation.

## Status

$\P$-complete (Kasai, Adachi, and Iwata [[1]](#1)).

## Remarks

The reduction to show hardness is from a logarithmic space bounded alternating Turing machine.

A pebble game is *solvable* if there exists $A \subseteq X$ such that $S \vdash^\ast A$ and $t \in A$. The *one-person pebble game*, which is to determine if the game is solvable, of fixed ranked is $\mathsf{NLOG}$-complete [[1]](#1). For questions with a similar flavor see [GAME]({{< relref "./a-11-1.md" >}}) and [CM]({{< relref "./a-11-2.md" >}}). For additional remarks about pebbling see [[2, Section 5.4]](#2).
